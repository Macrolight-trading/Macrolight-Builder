You launched your site three weeks ago. You've searched your business name, your services, your city — and you're nowhere. Not on page one. Not on page five. It's the most common question we get from new clients, and the honest answer has a range, not a date.

Here's what actually happens in the first 30, 90, and 180 days after a local business website goes live — and what determines whether you're closer to the fast end of the range or the slow end.

## The Short Answer

For a new local service business with no existing domain authority, expect this rough timeline:

- **Days 1–30:** You'll start ranking for your **business name** and very low-competition long-tail searches.
- **Days 30–90:** You'll start showing up for **moderate long-tail keywords** — usually 3–5 word service-and-city phrases.
- **Days 90–180:** You'll begin ranking for **mid-competition local terms** like "[service] near me" and "[service] in [city]," typically pages 2–3 first.
- **6–12 months:** You can realistically reach **page one** for your core service-and-city terms — if the site is built right and you're consistently doing the work.

Most local service businesses see meaningful lead flow from organic search start to materialize around the **4–6 month mark.** Anyone promising you page one in 30 days is either lying or planning to do something that will get you penalized.

## Why It Takes That Long

Google doesn't trust a new site instinctively. It needs to see three things, and they take time to develop:

### 1. Crawl and Index

Google has to actually find your pages, read them, and add them to its index. For a brand-new site with no inbound links, this can take a few days to a few weeks. Submitting a sitemap through Google Search Console can shorten this to days.

### 2. Topical Relevance

Google needs to figure out what your site is *about.* A roofer in Columbus with five thin pages looks identical to thousands of other sites until Google sees consistent signals: service-specific pages, location mentions, schema markup, internal links, and content that actually answers questions homeowners ask.

### 3. Trust and Authority

This is the slow part. Google measures trust through inbound links from real sites, mentions of your business name and address across the web (citations), reviews on your Google Business Profile, and engagement signals from real visitors. New sites have none of these on day one. Building them is a 6–12 month project, not a 30-day project.

## What Speeds Ranking Up

The fast end of the timeline isn't luck — it's the result of doing specific things from day one.

**Technical foundations from launch.** Fast load times (under 2 seconds on mobile), mobile-first design, valid schema markup, a clean sitemap, and zero crawl errors. If Google can't easily understand your site, nothing else matters.

**A real Google Business Profile, fully completed.** Verified address, accurate business hours, every service category filled in, weekly photos, and answers to the Q&A section. The Google Business Profile is the single biggest local ranking lever — and it's free.

**Dedicated pages for each service and each city you serve.** A plumber serving Columbus, Dublin, and Westerville should have at least three city pages, each genuinely different — not the same content with the city name swapped. Google detects template spam instantly.

**Real, ongoing reviews.** A site with 40 Google reviews averaging 4.8 stars will outrank a site with 4 reviews almost every time. Asking every happy customer for a review the day the job ends compounds fast.

**Consistent NAP citations.** Your business name, address, and phone number need to match exactly across your website, Google Business Profile, Yelp, Bing Places, Apple Maps, and the industry directories that matter for your trade. Inconsistent citations confuse Google and slow trust-building.

**A small amount of content, consistently.** Two thoughtful blog posts a month targeting real customer questions ("how long does a roof replacement take," "do I need a permit for a water heater install in [city]") signals topical relevance and pulls in long-tail traffic while you wait for the bigger keywords.

## What Quietly Stalls Ranking

Most stalled sites aren't stalled because of one big problem — they're stalled because of three or four small ones compounding.

**Thin, duplicated service pages.** If your roofing page and your gutters page share 80% of the same text with one paragraph swapped, Google treats them as near-duplicates and ranks neither.

**Slow mobile load times.** A 6-second mobile load isn't just a conversion problem — it's a ranking problem. Google's Core Web Vitals are an explicit ranking factor.

**No internal linking.** Pages that aren't linked to from anywhere else on your site get treated as low-priority. Your homepage should link to every service page; every service page should link to relevant city pages and blog posts.

**Missing or broken schema markup.** LocalBusiness schema with proper address, geo coordinates, hours, and service area tells Google exactly what you are and where you serve. Most small business sites either skip it entirely or have invalid markup that Google ignores.

**No Google Search Console or analytics setup.** If you're not measuring impressions, clicks, average position, and which pages Google is actually showing in search, you're guessing at what to fix.

**Buying links or stuffing keywords.** Both will either do nothing or actively hurt you. Modern Google is very good at detecting both.

## A Realistic 6-Month Trajectory

Here's what a well-built local business website typically looks like at each checkpoint:

**Month 1:** Site is indexed. Ranks #1 for the exact business name. Ranks somewhere in the top 100 for a handful of long-tail service phrases. 5–15 organic visitors per week.

**Month 3:** Ranking on page 1–2 for 5–10 long-tail keywords. Google Business Profile is showing up in the local map pack for some "[service] near me" searches when the user is physically close to the business. 30–60 organic visitors per week. First few leads from organic search start arriving.

**Month 6:** Page 1 for several mid-competition local terms. Map pack visibility for the core service in the home city. 100–250 organic visitors per week. 5–15 inbound leads per month from organic search. Total organic lead flow now meaningfully exceeds the monthly cost of the site.

**Month 12:** Page 1 for most core service-and-city terms. Strong map pack presence in the primary city and 1–2 adjacent cities. 300–700 organic visitors per week for an established local service business. Organic search is now the largest lead source, ahead of referrals and paid ads.

These numbers vary by industry and city size — a roofer in a metro of 2 million sees different volume than a plumber in a town of 30,000 — but the shape of the curve is consistent.

## When to Worry

If you're 6 months in and seeing none of the milestones above, something is wrong. The usual culprits, in order of how often we see them:

1. The site was never properly indexed — check Google Search Console coverage.
2. The site is technically slow or breaking Core Web Vitals on mobile.
3. The service pages are too thin or too duplicated to rank.
4. The Google Business Profile isn't claimed, verified, or fully filled out.
5. There are zero or near-zero Google reviews.
6. The business has inconsistent NAP across major directories.

Each of these is fixable. None of them are fast — but a focused 60-day push on the top two or three usually moves the needle within the following 90 days.

## The Bottom Line

A new local business website doesn't rank because it exists. It ranks because Google can crawl it, understand it, trust it, and verify that real customers like working with you. Those signals take months to develop even when you do everything right.

The businesses that win at local SEO aren't the ones with the biggest budget or the slickest design. They're the ones who set the foundations correctly on launch day and then do small, consistent work — a new review, a new service page, a new blog post answering a real customer question — for the next 12 months.

If you launched a site and the leads aren't coming, the answer is almost never "give up on SEO." It's usually "audit what you launched and fix the two or three things actually blocking ranking."
